# ZAP Blog Content Strategy & Publishing Playbook

---

## 1. Target Audience

ZAP's blog readership falls into four overlapping groups. Understanding who is actually reading informs keyword selection, content depth, and tone.

**TRT Patients (the largest segment)**
Men who have been prescribed testosterone replacement therapy, are newly starting, or are months/years into a protocol. Range from 30–65, often diagnosed by their primary care physician or through a TRT clinic. They have real medical questions, real blood work they're trying to interpret, and almost no one explaining things clearly to them. They are the most engaged readers and the highest-intent ZAP app users. They read slowly and carefully.

**Peptide Researchers (the most technically demanding segment)**
People who have done enough reading to know what BPC-157, ipamorelin, TB-500, and semax are. They have a research mindset, are skeptical of vague claims, and will leave immediately if the content is bro-science. They want dosing protocols, mechanism explanations, and practical guidance — not hype. They're also the segment most likely to be running multiple compounds simultaneously and most in need of a tracker.

**Serious Athletes and Performance Enthusiasts**
Intermediate to advanced gym-goers who are not (necessarily) on TRT but are interested in peptide protocols for recovery, body composition, or performance. BPC-157, TB-500, ipamorelin/CJC stacks, and GLP-1 peptides for body composition are their primary interests. They cross over substantially with the peptide researcher segment.

**Biohackers**
Self-experimenters interested in longevity, cognitive enhancement, and optimization. They're likely running multiple protocols across different categories simultaneously. They appreciate the data-driven, skeptical tone and are the most likely to try novel compounds like semax, selank, and epithalon.

**Tone note for all four:** Write for intelligent adults who are capable of handling nuance. Dry humor is welcome; condescension is not. Every post should make the reader feel like they're getting better information than they'd find anywhere else, delivered by someone who finds the subject genuinely interesting and is not managing their opinion for liability reasons.

## 2. Keyword Philosophy

**The core principle:** Target keywords where we can win — which means low-to-medium competition keywords with clear informational or commercial investigation intent. We are not competing for "testosterone" or "peptides." We are competing for "ipamorelin dosing protocol" and "how to reconstitute BPC-157" and "what to expect first month TRT."

These long-tail, specific keywords attract exactly the readers who are most likely to become ZAP users. Someone searching "how long does it take for TRT to work" is a week into a protocol. They need a tracker. We should be the article they find.

**Difficulty estimates** use a simplified scale:
- **Low:** Small number of high-quality competitors; achievable ranking within 3–6 months of posting
- **Med:** More established competitors but still winnable with superior content and consistent internal linking

## 4. The 8-Step Process for Writing a New Post

This is the repeatable checklist. Every post follows these steps, in this order.

---

**Step 1: Pick a keyword**
Use the cluster table in Section 2. Filter for Low difficulty first. Pick keywords with clear informational intent (someone asking a specific question) or commercial investigation intent (someone evaluating a compound or protocol). Avoid keywords where the top results are WebMD, Mayo Clinic, or large pharma sites — we are not competing there.

**Step 2: Define the keyword cluster**
For the chosen primary keyword, identify:
- 5–7 supporting keywords (related searches, longer-tail variations)
- 3 LSI (Latent Semantic Indexing) terms — related concepts that should appear naturally in the content to signal topical authority

Example for "ipamorelin dosing":
- Supporting: ipamorelin cjc 1295 stack, ipamorelin before bed, ipamorelin half life, ipamorelin protocol, growth hormone peptide protocol, gh secretagogue dosing
- LSI: pituitary, ghrelin receptor, pulsatile GH release

**Step 3: Write the title tag**
- Must include the primary keyword
- Must be under 60 characters (Google truncates at ~60)
- Should include a compelling secondary element: a number, a qualifier ("The Real Difference"), or a benefit phrase
- Format: `[Primary KW]: [Secondary Hook]`

**Step 4: Write the meta description**
- Must include the primary keyword
- Must include a clear benefit statement (what does the reader get from this article?)
- Under 155 characters
- Should avoid passive voice and generic phrases ("this article covers…")

**Step 5: Write the article outline**
Structure: H1 → 4–6 H2s → FAQ (5 questions) → CTA
- H1: Includes primary keyword, has a hook element (subhead, qualifier, dry wit angle)
- 4–6 H2s: Each targets one supporting keyword; each covers one distinct sub-topic
- FAQ: 5 questions that a real person searching the primary keyword would actually ask. These are FAQ schema targets for featured snippet capture.
- CTA: Always links to /app. One sentence of value prop specific to the post's topic.

**Step 6: Write the content**
- Minimum 1,600 words. Target 1,700–1,900 for most posts.
- Primary keyword must appear in the first 100 words of the article body (not the intro disclaimer)
- Primary keyword must appear in at least 2 H2s
- Supporting keywords must each appear naturally at least once in body text
- Every post must include at least one `<blockquote>` with a memorable, quotable sentence
- Tone: dry, precise, self-aware about the absurdity where relevant. No bro-science. No excessive hedging. If something is true, say it's true. If something is contested, say it's contested.
- Medical disclaimer at the top of every article body (verbatim from template)

**Step 7: Add all SEO infrastructure**
- JSON-LD Article schema (headline, description, url, datePublished, dateModified, author, publisher, mainEntityOfPage)
- JSON-LD BreadcrumbList schema (Home → Blog → Post Title)
- Open Graph tags (type, url, title, description, image, site_name)
- Twitter Card tags (card, title, description, image)
- `<link rel="canonical">` with the full https:// URL
- `<meta name="robots" content="index, follow">`

**Step 8: Add the post to all the right places**
1. Add URL to `sitemap.xml` with today's date as `lastmod`
2. Add blog card to `landing/blog/index.html` with appropriate tag, date, title, excerpt, and `read-more` link
3. If this is a top-priority post (high search volume or strong pillar anchor), add to the blog section on the landing page `landing/index.html`
4. Add internal links from 2+ existing related posts to the new post
5. Add 2+ internal links from the new post to existing related posts

**Additional internal linking guidelines:**
- Link on descriptive anchor text, not generic phrases like "click here" or "this article." Link on the exact concept being referenced: "your TRT blood work" not "read more about labs."
- Do not link to the same post more than twice in a single article.
- When a new post is published, go back and add a link to it from at least 2 existing relevant posts within the same week. The link web degrades quickly if you only link forward and never update older posts.
- The `/calculator.html` tool should be linked from any post that involves dosing math (reconstitution, peptide dosing, ester comparisons).
- The `/app` CTA link should appear at minimum once in the article body (contextually, not just the CTA block) and once in the CTA block at the bottom.

## 7. Publishing Frequency & Quality Bar

**Recommended cadence:** 1–2 posts per month. The quality bar is "best article on the internet about this specific topic" — not "passable article about this general topic." One excellent post that ranks is worth more than four adequate posts that don't.

**What "done" looks like before publishing:**

- [ ] All 8 steps in the writing process completed
- [ ] Word count is 1,600+ (count without HTML tags)
- [ ] Primary keyword appears in: H1, first 100 words, at least 2 H2s, meta description, title tag, JSON-LD headline
- [ ] 5 FAQ questions are complete, standalone, and actually answer the question asked
- [ ] Medical disclaimer is present at top of article body
- [ ] All JSON-LD schemas are valid (test at schema.org/docs/validator)
- [ ] Open Graph and Twitter Card tags are complete
- [ ] Canonical URL is correct
- [ ] At least 2 internal links to other posts, with descriptive anchor text
- [ ] At least 1 contextual link to /app in the article body
- [ ] CTA block is present with post-specific copy (not generic)
- [ ] Word count note at bottom with approximate count
- [ ] Added to sitemap.xml with correct lastmod date
- [ ] Card added to blog/index.html
- [ ] At least 2 existing posts updated to link to the new post
- [ ] Manually reviewed on mobile at 375px viewport width

**A note on timing:** publish when the post is actually ready. A post that sits at 90% done for two weeks is not adding SEO value. A post that ships at 100% quality builds the site's authority. The internal bar is: "would I be annoyed if a TRT clinic with a $50k content budget published this exact article and outranked us?" If yes, it's not ready.
